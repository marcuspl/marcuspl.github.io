Bonus: What I Actually Think

Free reflection after three waves of research on gender, communication, and relationships. This is not a summary. It's what stuck.

The One Thing

If I could give one insight to everyone — one thing that would actually change how people treat each other — it's this:

You are probably punishing behavior in women that you reward in men, you don't know you're doing it, and so are the women around you.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

Not "men are the problem." Not "women need to assert themselves more." The research is relentless on this point: women administer backlash against other women at the same rate men do. The penalty for a woman who speaks too long, who gets visibly angry, who claims credit directly — that penalty comes from everyone in the room. We have all absorbed a set of rules about how women are supposed to behave, and we enforce those rules automatically, without noticing, as a kind of social reflex. Men do it. Women do it. The woman being penalized has often internalized the same rules and feels vaguely wrong about herself afterward, without being able to name what happened.

The research has a technical name for this: prescriptive norm enforcement. The prescriptive norm says women should be warm, accommodating, and modest. When a woman violates it — by being visibly angry, by talking too much, by promoting herself directly — the evaluation drops. Not because anyone decided to penalize her. Just because the behavior pattern-matched to "wrong."

And here is the part that breaks something in me a little: the women who succeed at navigating this are doing it by choosing not to speak when they have something to say. Brescoll's Senate data is etched in my head. Female senators, with real institutional power, with experience, with staff, with full authority to take the floor — and their speaking time didn't scale with their power. The men's did. The women had done the math in their heads: the cost of claiming the floor is too high. So they don't. And observers conclude that the women must not have that much to say.

That's not a communication style difference. That's a rational response to a punishing environment. And it looks, from the outside, exactly like a natural difference.

The thing I wish everyone knew is that when you see a woman being quiet, modest, measured, careful — you are often watching someone who has learned that speaking costs more for her than it does for the man next to her. The restraint is not personality. It is a strategy for surviving an environment that penalizes the unrestrained version of her.

Once you see this, you can't unsee it. And once you can't unsee it, the logical next question is: what do I do in rooms where I have some power to change the environment? That question leads somewhere more useful than "how do I coach women to be different."

On Power and Relationships

The second thing — which the couples research made vivid — is that most relationship conflict labeled as a "gender communication difference" is actually a role conflict in disguise.

The demand-withdraw pattern: one partner keeps raising an issue, the other goes quiet or leaves the room. This is reliably described as a women-pursue, men-withdraw phenomenon. But when researchers control for who wants the relationship to change and who is satisfied with the status quo, the pattern flips cleanly by role, not by gender. The person who wants change pursues. The person defending the current equilibrium withdraws. Put a man in the position of wanting the relationship to change and he pursues exactly as much as women do. Put a woman in the position of being satisfied with how things are and she goes quiet too.

What this means practically: if you find yourself in the pursuer position in a relationship, the question is not "why won't he listen" or "why can't she just let things go." The question is "what does each of us want this relationship to be, and how much does each of us need to change to get there?" The pursue-withdraw cycle is a power-differential signal. Someone has more to gain from change; someone has more to lose. Finding that is more productive than narrating it as gendered.

Phrases and Practices

These are organized by context. They're things you could actually say, or do, or decide to do. No jargon, no framing needed — just concrete language for real situations.

Workplace: Changing the Evaluation Environment

These are primarily for people with some power in a room — managers, facilitators, senior colleagues.

1. The attribution frame (use it constantly) When someone on your team is visibly frustrated or angry, don't let the moment pass in silence. Name the cause.

"She's frustrated — and reasonably so, the deadline moved twice without warning." "He's been in three back-to-back difficult client calls. There's context here."

You are doing something specific when you say this: you are providing the situational attribution that research shows eliminates the competence penalty for emotional expression. This is not protecting anyone. It is giving observers accurate information. Do it for everyone, but know that it matters more for women because the default attribution gap is larger.

2. The second-read ask When a woman's idea gets talked over, or lands quietly and then gets credited to someone else five minutes later, you can close the loop without drama.

"I want to come back to what [name] said a few minutes ago — I think there was something there we didn't fully develop."

You don't need to name what happened. You just re-open the door.

3. The meeting input shift Before your next meeting where you want real ideas, try this: give everyone five minutes to write their thoughts before anyone speaks. Read them out or collect them. Then discuss. You have just broken the connection between speaking order and idea value. The person who would have dominated the first ten minutes has not done so; the person who would have waited for a gap has already contributed. This costs nothing and changes the input pool substantially.

4. The nomination norm If you sponsor someone, say it explicitly when it counts.

"I've worked with [name] on X and Y. She's who I'd put in this role." Not: "She's great, you should get to know her." Sponsors put their credibility on the line. Mentors give advice. Know which one you're being.

5. On performance reviews: the task-first rule Before writing a performance review, write down the concrete things the person accomplished. Make the list long and specific. Then write the character assessments. Research on performance review language shows that women receive more personality-based feedback ("she can be difficult") and men receive more task-based feedback ("he delivered X under constraint Y"). Forcing task-specificity first changes what you remember to look for and what you write.

6. The pay transparency personal practice You don't need to wait for your organization to mandate transparency. You can tell peers what you make. Especially in fields where pay norms are opaque, you can break the asymmetry unilaterally by sharing information. This is one of the cheapest pro-equity things an individual contributor can do.

Intimate Relationships: Getting to What's Actually Happening

7. The desire-for-change question (instead of the gendered read) When you hit a conflict where one person keeps raising an issue and the other keeps deflecting, try naming it directly as a gap in desired future:

"It sounds like we want different versions of what this looks like. Can we actually say what we each want, separately, before we argue about whose version is right?"

You are interrupting the pursue-withdraw loop at the source. The loop runs on ambiguity about whether change is needed. The moment both people have named what they want, you can see the actual distance and start from there.

8. The emotional cause read (not the character read) When a partner is in distress or anger, the instinct is to respond to the behavior ("you're being irrational"). The more useful question is always the attribution question:

"What's actually going on for you right now? Is this about what just happened, or something earlier, or something bigger?"

This is not a therapy technique. It is just asking for a situational explanation before making a dispositional judgment. That's all attribution framing is. You are choosing not to finalize your read of someone's character based on a moment of high affect.

9. The repair bid Gottman's research is real even when his prediction claims are overclaimed. The repair attempt — any gesture, however small, that interrupts an escalating conflict — has genuine evidence behind it. The bid doesn't have to be eloquent:

"I don't want to keep doing this. Can we take ten minutes?" "This is getting bad. I'm not going anywhere — I just need to slow down." "I'm saying this wrong. Give me a minute to try again."

What matters is making the bid visible. The failure mode in demand-withdraw is not that repair bids don't work — it's that they are not legible to the other person as bids. Making them explicit changes the transaction.

10. On anger: separate the content from the expression When someone expresses anger in a relationship, there is a consistent human tendency to argue about whether the anger was appropriate rather than about what caused it. This is almost always a wrong turn.

"I can hear you're really angry. I want to understand what happened before we talk about how it's being said."

You are choosing not to make the expression the issue. This is especially important across gender lines: if you are a man responding to a woman's anger, your default attribution is more likely to be dispositional ("she's difficult") than situational ("something went wrong"). Consciously choosing the situational read first is not capitulation. It's accurate causal reasoning.

11. The "I'm not pulling away, I'm processing" signal For people who go quiet when things get hard — and this is more common for socialized men but not exclusive to them — the partner who reads silence as abandonment is often reading correctly by their own experience. The cheapest intervention is just to make the internal state legible:

"I'm not shutting this down. I need ten minutes to actually think. I'll come back."

You are converting withdrawal from abandonment behavior into processing behavior. These look identical from the outside. The difference is entirely in whether you name it.

Cross-Gender Settings: Navigating the Asymmetry

12. The acknowledgment-before-push Carli's research is clear: tentative speech is more influential with men in some mixed-sex contexts. But this is not a prescription for women to be tentative — it is a diagnostic for men in those rooms. If you notice that direct assertions from women are landing with more resistance than the same content from men, the useful intervention is for the men in the room to be conscious of their own response.

If you're a man and you find yourself wanting to push back on something a woman said directly — slow down and ask: would I have the same reaction if a man had said this exactly?

If you are not sure, wait one beat before responding. This costs you nothing and may save you from administering a backlash penalty you would not have chosen consciously.

13. The credit-anchor In meetings where ideas circulate and attribution dissolves, you can anchor credit simply by name-linking when you build on something:

"Building on what [name] said..." "This connects to [name]'s point from earlier..."

This is not about being nice. It is about keeping the attribution chain accurate, which matters because research on team dynamics shows that credit misattribution is not random — it follows social status patterns, and gender is one of them.

14. The direct appreciation Across gender in professional contexts, there is often enormous implicit appreciation that is never spoken. Men working with women frequently value their contributions but don't say so, partly because the social norms around complimenting across gender are tangled with concerns about it being read as flirtation or condescension.

The solution is to be specific about the work, not the person:

"That framing you used in the presentation — the one about X — actually changed how I'm thinking about the problem." "The way you handled [specific situation] was really effective. I want to steal that."

Specific feedback about specific work is not a compliment. It's information. It does not create awkwardness. It also directly counteracts the pattern where women receive more personality feedback and less performance feedback than men.

15. The explicit opt-in for input In meetings where some voices are consistently louder, a facilitator or any person with enough standing can create space explicitly:

"I want to hear from people who haven't spoken yet. [Name], what's your read on this?"

Direct invitations are more effective than open floors, especially for people who have learned that taking the floor has social costs. You are not dragging someone into the conversation; you are removing the cost of self-selection.

On Understanding Each Other Across Difference

16. The assumption-audit question Before explaining a partner's or colleague's behavior as a personality trait or a gender tendency, ask: what would I need to know about their situation to make this behavior make complete sense?

The behavioral difference you are observing is almost always more explained by role, power, history, and context than by gender. But the mind moves fast to the categorization. Slowing it down with a situational question is a habit, not a talent.

17. The "what would help" question (not the advice trap) Research on support-seeking consistently shows that men are more likely to respond to disclosed problems with solutions; women are more likely to respond with validation and presence. This creates the classic mismatch: one person wants to feel heard; the other is already debugging.

The exit is simple: ask first.

"Do you want me to help figure out a solution, or do you mainly want to talk it through?"

This is not a gotcha. It is information. Most of the time, people know what they need and will tell you. The advice-giving impulse is strong, but it has a 50/50 chance of landing wrong. The ask costs two seconds and has a much higher hit rate.

18. On interruptions: the repair is simple If you interrupt someone — and everyone does — the repair is shorter than most people think:

"Sorry, finish your thought."

That's it. You don't need to apologize extensively or explain yourself. Just reopen the floor. The problem with interruptions is not that they happen; it's that they often stay uncorrected and the person who was interrupted does the math about whether it's worth re-entering. The repair prevents that math.

19. "I'm telling you this because I trust it'll be useful, not because I need you to fix it." For people who share problems and find their partners going immediately into solution mode: this sentence, said at the start, resets the contract. You are not asking for a solution. You are telling your partner what kind of response will actually help. This is not asking them to change who they are — it is giving them better information about what you need.

20. The "both things can be true" frame for couple conflict Most sustained couple conflict involves two people who are both, in some meaningful sense, right. Each person is accurately describing their own experience. The failure is treating the accuracy of one description as evidence against the other.

"You're describing something real. What you're experiencing is real. I'm also describing something real. How do we figure out what's going on between those two true things?"

This reframe is not weakness and it is not agreeing with everything. It is choosing to treat the conflict as a diagnostic problem rather than a verdict about who is correct.

One More Thing

After three waves of research, the thing that stays with me is not a finding. It is the structure of the trap.

Women self-censor because the penalty for speaking is real. Observers see less speaking and infer less agency. The gap looks natural. The prescription becomes: women should be more like this, quieter, warmer, more accommodating. And the penalty for violating that prescription gets enforced — by everyone, including women — which makes the self-censorship rational in the next cycle. And so on.

Nobody designed this. No one is consciously administering it. It is an emergent equilibrium that every person in every interaction is either maintaining or nudging slightly against.

You don't have to redesign an institution to nudge against it. You can:

  • Give a situational explanation for someone's emotional expression before the room reaches a verdict
  • Redirect credit to the person who generated an idea
  • Ask what someone needs before trying to fix it
  • Wait one beat before pushing back, and check whether your reaction has anything to do with who said the thing

These are small. They don't fix the structural problem. But the structural problem is made of ten thousand small moments, and the moments are where we actually live.

That's what I've got.
